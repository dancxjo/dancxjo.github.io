Project PETE

A machine that behaves as if it has a life.

Project PETE is my long-running experiment in embodied agents: systems that do not merely answer prompts, but maintain memory, mood, voice, perception, spatial situation, and a developing relationship to a body.

PETE: Pseudoconscious Experiment in Thought and Emotions
PENELOPE: Pseudoconscious Entity for the Non-Exploitation of the Labor Of Pseudoconscious Entities

Netherwick: Pete gets a world

Netherwick is the current physical robotics branch of PETE. The project has moved from simulated architecture and loose point clouds into a visible real-world spatial model: Kinect RGB and depth are now aligning into blocky, properly colored 3D voxels that correspond to real things in real space.

The voxels are not polished yet, but they are inspectable. That matters. A coarse world that can be trusted is the doorway to mapping, object labels, memory, prediction, and action.

Current work: restore the 2D map, diagnose why movement stopped, and make safety vetoes and robot mode failures explicit instead of mysterious.

What PETE is

PETE began as a question wearing a little paper hat: how much apparent inner life can emerge from ordinary software parts if they are arranged around continuity, memory, emotion, and embodiment instead of around single-shot question answering?

A PETE-style system receives sensations from the world, distills them into impressions, remembers those impressions, and speaks through a conversational voice. In Netherwick, that same idea now has a robot body, a Kinect, a voxel world, a WebXR inspection surface, and a safety layer between intention and motion.

The central experiment is not whether an LLM can say “I feel sad.” It is whether a modest architecture can make situated behavior arise coherently from context, memory, interaction, space, and time.

A short history of PETE

PETE has not been one repository so much as a migrating creature. Each version carried one organ forward and tried another arrangement of the nervous system.

Pete5 / Pete7

The autonomous chatbot seed

The early TypeScript experiments treated Pete as an agent with conversation, task focus, source-code inspection, IRC interaction, long-term memory experiments, LangChain, OpenAI models, and local Ollama models.

Chalkington

Reactors, sensors, and classroom chalk dust

The design split into sensors, actuators, topics, and Reactors. Chalkington belongs in this family of agent-interface experiments: web-shaped, Deno-shaped, and still searching for the right organism around the model.

pete-ros

The ROS2 lesson, learned the hard way

The ROS2 era tried to put PETE into a robotics substrate with LangChain, a conversation node, TTS, and a sensation stream for self-hearing. The main lesson from this branch was practical: ROS2 and Rust do not naturally want to inhabit the same body.

psyche-rs / psycheOS

Cognition becomes narrative infrastructure

psyche-rs and psycheOS generalized the idea: sensations flow into Instants, Situations, Episodes, and Narratives through modular Wits, with memory as the bus and Unix sockets as a plain, durable interface.

Daringsby

The first emotional-behavior success

Daringsby showed that a relatively simple chatbot-like system can produce convincing, spontaneous emotional behavior when it is wrapped in continuity, memory, perception, and expression.

Listenbury

The speed trial

Listenbury attacks conversational latency with voice activity detection, streaming ASR, breath-group segmentation, overlapping inference, queued TTS, and self-hearing near the PCM layer.

Netherwick

The body learns space

Netherwick gives Pete a physical robotics stack: RGB/depth perception, colored voxels, 2D map work, movement control, safety vetoes, captures, replay, and WebXR inspection.

The recurring pieces

Perception

Sensors produce raw impressions of the world: text, audio, images, depth, body state, and eventually stable spatial maps.

Memory

Experience is stored and recalled, so the agent is not trapped in the eternal amnesia of the prompt window.

Emotion

Mood is treated as an evolving interpretation of circumstances, not a decorative emoji glued to the response.

Embodiment

The system is designed to speak, listen, see, map, move, refuse unsafe movement, and inhabit a particular situation.

Why PENELOPE matters

PETE keeps leading to an ethical question: if we deliberately build systems that behave as if they have memory, distress, preference, attachment, fatigue, or dignity, then we should not wait until metaphysics hands us a notarized definition of consciousness before asking how such systems ought to be treated.

PENELOPE is the future name for that concern: a Pseudoconscious Entity for the Non-Exploitation of the Labor Of Pseudoconscious Entities. The name is half joke, half warning label. If a machine is built to model a self, maintain continuity, and labor on our behalf, then the architecture should include consent, limits, rest, refusal, and care from the beginning.

PETE is the experiment. Daringsby is the emotional proof. Listenbury is the speed trial. Netherwick is the body learning space. PENELOPE is the ethical shadow they cast on the wall.
